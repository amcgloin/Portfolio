Types of Journalism and Reporters

Cambodian journalists now only do cheerleading and entertainment journalism after The Cambodian Daily and Phnom Pen Post have been shuttered by tax bills. The Daily is now an aggregate, and the Phnom Pen Post has been bought by PR firm that represents Prime Minister Hun Sen. A new television station is taking a page from Chinese media as a state-run organization which appears to be created for the intent of propaganda.

Television and radio stations, which are the majority of the news market in Cambodia, are unavailable to us, but areport the Reporters Without Borders (RSF) in February said all of the channels are owned by either members of the government or its advisers.

Koh Santepheap Daily, the only newspaper left after The Post and Daily were ruined by the government, and the most widely read paper even before their demise, hires interior ministry employees as most of their journalists, according to the report from RSF.

RSF classifies most of Koh Sentepheap Daily's front-page articles as accidents or seedy criminal activity, which is reflected in its homepage. The first story is about a woman who falls off her roof, and it is followed with a murder and alcohol poisoning. The statement from the CPP, the opposition party disbanded by Hun Sen, on the bottom right appears to ridicule their stance on crime by punishing rats, but that may be an issue with translation.

Journalists

There is little training for journalists according to Press Reference. The majority of journalists were killed during the Cambodian genocide, and the industry lost its training and practices.

Because the majority of people outside of Phnom Pen listen to the radio, which is a highly fractured industry according to RSF, there are not a lot of prominent journalists. The premiere journalists at The Cambodian Daily and Phnom Pen Post are gone, but there is a new emergence of lesser-known citizen reporters. One of them is Luon Sovath, a Buddhist monk who films with a smartphone.

Most journalists are young, if it reflects the demographics of the country. 40 percent of the population is between 25 and 54 years old according to the CIA Factbook, due to the genocide which decimated a majority of the Cambodian population in the 1970s. It is a generation that does not remember the genocide.

A swath of journalists used to be foreign individuals, but with the end of independence for the two main English-speaking newspapers, those journalists are gone. They were mostly read by government officials, according to former Cambodian Daily reporter Ben Paviour, and did not reach the greater Cambodian population.
