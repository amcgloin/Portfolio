My choice

While Cambodia is mostly safe in terms of physical well-being, it is known for threatening and jailing journalists. detention of two Radio Free Asia journalists for six months with a possible penalty of 15 years in prison under charges of supplying information to a foreign state.

Secret police harassed and arrested journalists and commentators in 2017, James Ricketson, an Australian filmmaker, has been jailed for the past year after flying a drone, and may face a criminal trial unless a judge decides to drop the charges against him, according to MSN. and 30-year-journalism-veteran Aun Pheap, who wrote for The Cambodian Daily, had to leave Cambodia after being charged with inciting crime regarding his election coverage last year.

Past events

Journalists used to be in more danger. July 2016 the political analyst Kem Ley was killed in what appeared to be a hit from the Cambodian government, according to The Cambodian Daily.

Four years ago, a reporter was killed investigating logging by a police chief, a military police officer and a Cambodia Royal Air Force member who were suspected of trafficking lumber according to Reporters Without Borders.

Conclusion

Cambodia does not appear to be a good place for a journalist, especially a foreign journalist. All the foreign journalists in the country are either in jail, or have fled. The major news organizations for English-speaking reporters have also been closed down, with oppressive taxes the state has claimed they owe.

Even experienced foreign reporters, such as Ben Paviour of The Cambodian Daily and Aun Pheap have decided to leave the country in fear of being jailed by the government.
